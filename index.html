The Ideology of the Supreme Court

By: Joshua Ip, Karamvir Rai, Nan Lin, Sherley Thirumavalan

The Supreme Court of the United States is simultaneously one of the most powerful and one of the least understood branches of American government. Since the 1900s, the nine Justices of the court have been responsible for some of the biggest shifts in American politics. From the end of segregation to the beginning of the Citizens United era, the court has evolved both politically and ideologically.

The Supreme Court has the power of Judicial Review, where it can decide what the law means and whether or not a particular law is constitutional. In the Supreme Court, four justices must vote in order to take up a court case. Once the case is argued, a majority of the nine justices must sign on to an opinion in order for it to become binding.

1937: National Labor Relations Board v Jones & Laughlin Steel Corporation

This was a United States Supreme Court case that upheld the constitutionality of the National Labor Relations Act of 1935, also known as the Wagner Act. The case represented a major expansion in the Court's interpretation of Congress's power under the Commerce Clause and effectively spelled the end to the Court's striking down of New Deal economic legislation.
1938: New Negro Alliance v. Sanitary Grocery Co.

New Negro Alliance v. Sanitary Grocery Co., was a landmark decision of the US Supreme Court which affects US labor law, safeguarding a right to boycott and in the struggle by African Americans against discriminatory hiring practices.
1939: United States v. Miller

This was a landmark decision of the US Supreme Court that involved a Second Amendment challenge to the National Firearms Act of 1934 (NFA). The case is often cited in the ongoing US gun politics debate, as both sides claim that it supports their position.
1940: Chambers v. Florida

This was a landmark United States Supreme Court case that dealt with the extent to which police pressure resulting in a criminal defendant's confession violates the Due Process clause.The court held that the use of mental torture, accompanied by threats of violence, was enough to justify the suppression of a confession.

1941: The Stone Court Begins

Following the retirement of Chief Justice Evan Hughes, President Franklin Delano Roosevelt nominates Associate Justice Harlan Stone as the next chief justice. Stone is considerably more liberal than his predecessor. As Chief Justice, he has the ability to set the initial agenda for the Supreme Court and to assign which justice will write the Court's opinion for a particular case. This power allows Chief Justices to influence the historical record.
1942: Glasser v. United States

In a 7-2 decision, the Court ruled that excluding women from a jury pool was unconstitutional. The court stated that jury pools should be a "cross-section of the community."
1943: West Virginia State Board of Education v. Barnette

In a 6-3 decision written by Justice Jackson, the court rules that the Free Speech Clause of the first amendment prevents schools from forcing students to salute the American flag or recite the Pledge of Allegiance.
1944: Korematsu v. United States

In a 6-3 decision, the court rules that the relocation of Japanese Americans from their homes to military camps is constitutional. Roosevelt's Executive Order 9066 ordered "all persons of Japanese ancestry, including aliens and non-aliens" to be relocated to internment camps. It is widely considered to be one of the most racist rulings in American History.
1945: Associated Press v. United States

The Supreme Court in Associated Press v. United States upheld an injunction filed against the Associated Press for alleged violations of the Sherman Antitrust Act of 1890. Most of the violations related to the agreement that AP members made not to share their stories with nonmembers and to the power that existing members had to exclude new ones.
1946: The Vinson Court Begins

Vinson took office at a time when the court was divided into two camps: a progressive camp led by Justices Black and Douglas, and a more conservative camp led by Justices Jackson and Frankfurter. President Roosevelt had appointed justices who would uphold the more expansive economic regulations of the New Deal, but the same Roosevelt appointees often split on civil liberties cases. On his death, The New York Times credited Vinson for soothing the tensions between the two blocs of justices, but legal historian Michal Belknap argues that Vinson was largely unsuccessful in this endeavor.
1947: Everson v. Board of Education

Everson v. Board of Education, was a landmark decision of the United States Supreme Court which applied the Establishment Clause in the country's Bill of Rights to state law. Prior to this decision, the First Amendment's words, "Congress shall make no law respecting an establishment of religion" imposed limits only on the federal government, and many states continued to grant certain religious denominations legislative or effective privileges.
1948: United States v. Paramount Pictures

United States v. Paramount Pictures, Inc., was a landmark United States Supreme Court antitrust case that decided the fate of film studios owning their own theatres and holding exclusivity rights on which theatres would show their movies. It would also change the way Hollywood movies were produced, distributed, and exhibited. The Supreme Court affirmed (a District Court's ruling) in this case that the existing distribution scheme was in violation of the United States antitrust law, which prohibit certain exclusive dealing arrangements.
1949: Truman Nominates Clark and Minton

Following the deaths of Justices Harlan Stone and Frank Murphy, Truman had the opportunity to nominate two new justices in a single year. Truman nominated Attorney General Tom Clark, a personal friend with no judicial experience, to the post. For the other seat, he nominated 7th Circuit Judge Sherman Minton, also a personal friend. Clark's nomination was controversial but ultimately the Democratic Senate confirmed him. Clark would serve for decades. Ideologically, both were considered moderates.
1950: Dean Milk Company v. City of Madison

In Dean Milk Company v. City of Madison, the court ruled that the Smith Act, a federal law which made it a crime to advocate or organize for the purpose of overthrowing the government, did not violate the First Amendment of the Constitution. Though overturned in 1969, it is one of the few times the court has placed substantial limitations on free speech.
1951

1952: Joseph Burstyn, Inc. v. Wilson

This case, also referred to as the Miracle Decision, was a landmark decision by the United States Supreme Court that largely marked the decline of motion picture censorship in the United States. In this case, the Supreme Court ruled that a New York education law allowing a film to be banned on the basis of its being sacrilegious violated the First Amendment.
1953: The Warren Court Begins

President Eisenhower nominates Associate Justice Earl Warren as the new Chief Justice. Warren had a more expansive view of the court's power than his predecessors. A liberal himself, during Warren's term the court would develop a solid liberal majority. The Warren Court is often considered to be the most liberal court in American history, and would lay down many landmark cases which would reshape everything from civil rights to federal power.
1954: Brown v. Board of Education

In this landmark decision, the U.S. Supreme Court ruled that U.S. state laws establishing racial segregation in public schools are unconstitutional, even if the segregated schools are otherwise equal in quality. The Court's unanimous (9–0) decision stated that "separate educational facilities are inherently unequal", and therefore violate the Equal Protection Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment of the U.S. Constitution.
1955: Harlan joins the Court

Following the death of justice Robert Jackson, Eisenhower nominated John Marshall Harlan II to replace him. The grandson of a former Supreme Court Justice, Harlan would prove to be a stauch conservative and a reliable ideological counterpart to the liberal bloc of the Warren Court. Though rarely influential enough to persuade a member of the liberal majority to defect, his tightly reasoned dissents would make him a leader in conservative jurisprudence.
1956: Brennan joins the Court

Justice William Brennan, appointed by Eisenhower, replaced Sherman Minton on the court. An outspoken progressive, he is widely considered to be a leader in the liberal wing and as well as a highly effective jurist. He would write many landmark case opinions, including Baker v. Carr and New York Times v. Sullivan.
1957: Reid v. Covert

In Reid v. Covert, the court ruled that United States citizens, even when abroad even when associated with the military and even when outside of the territorial jurisdiction of the United States, cannot be deprived of the protections of the Constitution.
1958: Kent v. Dullhes

In Kent v. Dullhes, the court ruled that the right to travel is a part of the "liberty" of which the citizen cannot be deprived without due process of law under the Fifth Amendment.
1959: Williams v. Lee

In Williams v. Lee, the court ruled that state courts do not have jurisdiction on Indian reservations without the authorization of Congress.
1960: Boynton v. Virginia

In Boynton v. Virginia, the court ruled that Racial segregation in all forms of public transportation is illegal under the Interstate Commerce Act of 1887.
1961: Mapp v. Ohio

In Mapp v. Ohio, the court ruled in a 5-3 vote that evidence seized unlawfully could not be used in criminal prosecutions in state courts.
1962: Baker v. Carr

In one of the most important cases of the century, the court that federal courts could intervene to change how legislative districts were drawn. In this case, Justice Brennan laid down the principle of "one person, one vote." It is still a guiding principle of American politics to this day.
1963: Gideon v. Wainwright

In a landmark case, Justice Black ruled that states must grant criminals a public defender if they are being tried for a crime in a state court. Previous jurisprudence held that public defenders were only required for federal criminal cases.
1964: Reynold v. Sims, Wesberry v. Sanders

In these two cases, the court put the "one person, one vote" principle in Baker v. Carr into practice.
The court ruled in Reynold v. Sims that state legislative districts must be drawn so that an equal number of voters are in each district. In Wesberry v. Sanders, the court decided that U.S congressional districts must also be drawn to have an equal number of voters in each district.
1965: Griswold v. Connecticut

In this case, the Supreme Court ruled that a state's ban on the use of contraceptives violated the right to marital privacy. This was in response to a 1879 law that criminalized the encouragement or use of birth control.
1966: Miranda v. Arizona

In this landmark Supreme Court case, the Court held that if police do not inform people they arrest about certain constitutional rights, including their Fifth Amendment right against self-incrimination, then their confessions may not be used as evidence at trial.
1967: Loving v. Virginia

Loving v. Virginia, was a landmark civil rights decision of the U.S. Supreme Court in which the Court ruled that laws banning interracial marriage violate the Equal Protection and Due Process Clauses of the Fourteenth Amendment to the U.S. Constitution. The decision was followed by an increase in interracial marriages in the U.S. and is remembered annually on Loving Day.
1968: Terry v. Ohio

1969: The Burger Court Begins

Following the retirement of Chief Justice Earl Warren, President Richard Nixon appointed Warren Burger as the next Chief Justice. Burger was significantly more conservative than Burger, though not as conservative as his successors Rhenquist and Roberts.
1970: Oregon v. Mitchell

In Oregon v. Mitchell, the court ruled that congress has the power to regulate requirements for voting in federal elections, but it is prohibited from regulating requirements for voting in state and local elections.
1971: New York Times Co. v. United States

New York Times Co. v. United States, was a landmark decision of the US Supreme Court on the First Amendment. The ruling made it possible for The New York Times and The Washington Post newspapers to publish the then-classified Pentagon Papers without risk of government censorship or punishment.
1972: William Rehnquist joins the Court

With the death of Harlan Stone, Richard Nixon nominated William Rehnquist to replace him. Immediately, Rehnquist established himself as one of, if not the, most conservative members of the court. As an intellectual leader and a future Chief Justice, his tenure on the court would lay the ideological groundwork for the Court's rightward shift in the latter half of the 20th century.
1973: Roe v. Wade

Roe v. Wade was a landmark decision of the U.S. Supreme Court in which the Court ruled that the Constitution of the United States protects a pregnant woman's liberty to choose to have an abortion without excessive government restriction.
1974: United States v. Nixon

United States v. Nixon was a landmark United States Supreme Court case that resulted in a unanimous decision against President Richard Nixon, ordering him to deliver tape recordings and other subpoenaed materials to a federal district court. The decision was important to the late stages of the Watergate scandal, when there was an ongoing impeachment process against Richard Nixon. United States v. Nixon is considered a crucial precedent limiting the power of any U.S. president to claim executive privilege.
1975: O'Connor v. Donaldson

In O'Connor v. Donaldson, the court ruled theatres The states cannot involuntarily commit individuals if they are not a danger to themselves or others and are capable of living by themselves or with the aid of responsible family members or friends.
1976: Buckley v. Valeo

In Buckley v. Valeo, the court ruled that spending money to influence elections is a form of constitutionally protected free speech; therefore, federal limits on campaign contributions are constitutional in only a limited number of circumstances. This ruling applied to private individuals and not businesses.
1977: Carey v. Population Services International

In Carey v. Population Services International, the court ruled that laws that restrict the sale, distribution, and advertisement of contraceptives to both adults and minors are unconstitutional.
1978: Regents of the University of California v. Bakke

In Regents of University of California v. Bakke (1978), the Supreme Court ruled that a university's use of racial "quotas" in its admissions process was unconstitutional, but a school's use of "affirmative action" to accept more minority applicants was constitutional in some circumstances. The case involved the admissions practices of the Medical School of the University of California at Davis.
1979: Addington v. Texas

In Addington v. Texas the court ruled a "clear and convincing" standard of proof is required by the Fourteenth Amendment in a civil proceeding brought under state law to commit an individual involuntarily for an indefinite period to a state mental hospital.
1980: Diamond v. Chakrabarty

In Diamond v. Chakrabarty, the court ruled that genetically modified organisms could be patented. This case helped open the way for modern bioengineering companies and genetically modified foods.

1982: Enmund v. Florida

In Enmund v. Florida, the court ruled that a death sentence may not be imposed on offenders who are involved in a felony during which a murder is committed but who do not actually kill, attempt to kill, or intend that a killing take place.
1983: Sony v. Universal

In Sony v. Universal, the court ruled that manufacturers of home video recording devices cannot be liable for copyright infringement by their purchasers because the devices are sold for legitimate purposes and have non-infringing uses. Personal use of the machines to record TV for later viewing constitutes fair use.

1986: The Rehnquist Court Begins

With the retirement of Warren Burger, Ronald Reagan elevated William Rehnquist to the Chief Justice and nominated Antonin Scalia to fill Rehnquist's vacated associate justice seat. Both men would come to be considered staunch conservatives and ideological leaders of the movement, with Scalia becoming one of the strongest champions of "originalism." In originalism, the constitution should be interpreted according to how it would have been originally understood at the time of its writing rather than reinterpreted to fit the changing times.
1987: Edwards v. Aguillard

In Edwards v. Aguillard, the court ruled that teaching creationism in public schools is unconstitutional.
1988: Hustler v. Falwell

In Hustler Magazine v. Falwell, the court ruled that parodies of public figures, including those intended to cause emotional distress, are protected by the First Amendment.
1989: Texas v. Johnson

Texas v. Johnson was a landmark decision of the US Supreme Court that invalidated prohibitions on desecrating the American flag. At the time, such laws were enforced in 48 of the 50 states.
1990: Cruzan v. Missouri Dept. of Health

In Cruzan v. Missouri Dept. of Health, the court ruled ruled that it was constitutional to require "clear and convincing evidence" of a patient's wishes for removal of life support. It was the first "right to die" case brought before the court.
1991: Feist Publications, Inc. v. Rural Telephone Service Company, Inc.

In this landmark copyright case, the court ruled that originality is required for a work to obtain copyright. Previously, the court had followed the doctrine of 'sweat of the brow,' in which the most important factor for recieving a copyright was whether or not the applicant had invested significant time and energy into their work. Originality is still the foundation of copyright law today.
1992: Planned Parenthood of Southeastern Pennsylvania v. Casey

In Casey v. Planned Parenthood, the Supreme Court affirmed the basic ruling of Roe v. Wade that the state is prohibited from banning most abortions. Casey also ruled, however, that states may regulate abortions so as to protect the health of the mother and the life of the fetus, and may outlaw abortions of "viable" fetuses.
1993: Ginsburg joins the court

After the retirement of Justice White, Bill Clinton nominated Ruth Bader Ginsburg to replace her. Already a feminist icon at the time for her career as a judge and lawyer, she would become a staunch liberal on the court. Her sharp opinions and ideological positions would earn her the nickname 'Notorious R.B.G'
1994: J. E. B. v. Alabama

In J.E.B v. Alabama, the court ruled that lawyers could not use preemptory challenges to remove members from a jury based on sex. In the case, the prosecution had used preemptory challenges to remove every male member of the jury.
1995: United States v. Lopez

In United States v. Lopez, the court ruled that the Gun-Free School Zones Act of 1990 is unconstitutional. The court found that Congress did not have the power to criminalize gun posession itself, even near a school.
1996: Romer v. Evans

In Romer v. Evans, the court ruled that a Colorado state constitutional amendment that prevented homosexuals and bisexuals from being able to obtain protections under the law is a violation of the Equal Protection Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment.
1997: Washington v. Glucksberg

In Washington v. Glucksberg, the court ruled that states could ban assisted suicide.
1998: Clinton v. City of New York

In Clinton v. City of New York, the court ruled that the Line Item Veto Act of 1996 is unconstitutional because it allows the President to amend or repeal parts of statutes without the pre-approval of Congress. Currently, the presidential veto can only be used on an entire legislation, rather than individual parts of a piece of legislation.
1999

2000: Bush v. Gore

Bush v. Gore was a controversial decision that settled a recount dispute in Florida's 2000 presidential election between George W. Bush and Al Gore. On December 8, the Florida Supreme Court had ordered a statewide recount of all undervotes, over 61,000 ballots that the vote tabulation machines had missed. In a per curiam decision, the Court first ruled, strictly on equal protection grounds, that the recount be stopped.
2001: A&M Records, Inc. v. Napster, Inc.

In this case, the court ruled that peer-to-peer (P2P) file-sharing service Napster could be held liable for the copyright infringement of its users. This case has forced torrenting services to set up their businesses outside the United States.
2002: Board of Education v. Earls

In Board of Education v. Earls, the court ruled that it was constitutional for a school district to force a student to take a drug test in order to participate in extracurricular activities.
2003: Lawrence v. Texas

In Lawrence v. Texas, the court ruled that a Texas law that criminalized consensual same-sex sexual conduct violated homosexuals' right to privacy under the Due Process Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment. This decision invalidated all remaining sodomy laws in the United States, and was a landmark in LGBT rights.
2004: Hamdi v. Rumseld

In Hamdi v. Rumsfeld, the court ruled that the federal government has the power to detain those it designates as enemy combatants, including United States citizens. 2005: The Roberts Court Begins

With the death of William Rehnquist, George W. Bush appointed John Roberts to replace him as Chief Justice. Especially following the Trump nominations, the Roberts court has been much more conservative than its predecessors. The present-day Court frequently uses originalism or textualism to justify its majority opinions while paying less deference to precedent than previous courts.
2006: Hamdan v. Rumsfeld

In Hamdan v. Rumsfeld, the court ruled that the military commissions set up by the Bush administration to try detainees at Guantanamo Bay detention camp were illegal because they lack the protections that are required by the Geneva Conventions and the Uniform Code of Military Justice.
2007: Massachusetts v. EPA

In Massachusetts v. Environmental Protection Agency, the court ruled that greenhouse gases are air pollutants, and the Environmental Protection Agency may regulate their emission under the Clean Air Act. This case opened the way for the EPA to play a larger role in reducing greenhouse gas emissions and limiting climate change.
2008: District of Columbia v. Heller

This is the case in which the U.S. Supreme Court held that the Second Amendment guarantees an individual right to possess firearms independent of service in a state militia and to use firearms for traditionally lawful purposes, including self-defense within the home. It was the first Supreme Court case to explore the meaning of the Second Amendment since United States v. Miller(1939).
2009

2010: Citizens United v. Federal Election Commission

Citizens United v. Federal Election Commission, was a landmark decision of the Supreme Court of the United States concerning campaign finance. The Court held that the free speech clause of the First Amendment prohibits the government from restricting independent expenditures for political communications by corporations, including nonprofit corporations, labor unions, and other associations.
2011: Brown v. Entertainment Merchants Association

In Brown v. Entertainment Merchants Association, the court ruled that it was unconsitutional for the law to ban the sale of violent video games games to children without parental supervision.
2012: National Federation of Independent Business v. Sebelius

National Federation of Independent Business v. Sebelius, was a landmark United States Supreme Court decision in which the Court upheld Congress' power to enact most provisions of the Patient Protection and Affordable Care Act (ACA), commonly called Obamacare, and the Health Care and Education Reconciliation Act (HCERA), including a requirement for most Americans to have health insurance by 2014. The Acts represented a major set of changes to the American health care system that had been the subject of highly contentious debate, largely divided on political party lines.
2013: Shelby County v. Holder

This was a decision of the US Supreme Court regarding the constitutionality of two provisions of the Voting Rights Act of 1965: Section 5, which requires certain states and local governments to obtain federal preclearance before implementing any changes to their voting laws or practices; and Section 4(b), which contains the coverage formula that determines which jurisdictions are subjected to preclearance based on their histories of discrimination in voting. On June 25, 2013, the Supreme Court ruled that the coverage formula in Section 4(b) of the Voting Rights Act is unconstitutional because it is based on an old formula.
2014: Burwell v. Hobby Lobby Stores, Inc.

Burwell v. Hobby Lobby, is a landmark decision in corporate law by the United States Supreme Court allowing closely held for-profit corporations to be exempt from a regulation its owners religiously object to, if there is a less restrictive means of furthering the law's interest, according to the provisions of the Religious Freedom Restoration Act.
2015: Obergefell v. Hodges

This is a landmark civil rights case in which the Supreme Court of the United States ruled that the fundamental right to marry is guaranteed to same-sex couples by both the Due Process Clause and the Equal Protection Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment to the United States Constitution.
2016: A Vacancy on the Court

Justice Antonin Scalia, a conservative leader on the court, died of natural causes in 2016. Though Obama was the president at that time, the Republican-controlled Senate refused to consider Obama's nominees.
2017: Gorsuch replaces Scalia

With the death of Antonin Scalia in 2016, the court had been missing a justice for almost two years. With Donald Trump as the new president, the Senate confirmed Neil Gorsuch to fill the vacant seat. The Senate changed the rules so that Supreme Court nominees could not be filibustered, changing the number of votes which were needed from 60 to 51. Though Gorsuch was ideologically similar to Scalia, the refusal to consider Obama's nominees led to a new level of hostility between the parties on SCOTUS nominations.
2018: Kavanaugh replaces Kennedy

With the retirement of Anthony Kennedy, Donald Trump nominated Brett Kavanaugh to replace him. This was a notable shift in the modern court's composition because Kavanaugh was expected to be substantially more conservative than Kennedy. Kennedy had previously been the median justice on the court, so Gorsuch's nomination created a new conservative majority on the court.
2019: Department of Homeland Security v. Regents of the University of California

In Department of Homeland Security v. Regents of the University of California, the court ruled that a 2017 U.S. Department of Homeland Security (DHS) order to rescind the Deferred Action for Childhood Arrivals (DACA) immigration program was "arbitrary and capricious" under the Administrative Procedure Act (APA) and reversed the order.
The last 80 years of the Supreme Court's history have been eventful, but the ideological shift of the court continues in the present day. With the death of liberal icon Ruth Bader Ginsburg and Donald Trump's nomination of conservative judge Amy Coney Barrett to replace her, the court is expect to shift even further to the right.
